Work here in three registers: 
1. A source-extraction brief answers to a specific paper and reports what that paper proves, what it states without our having independently checked, and what we have inferred from reading it. 
2. A search memo tracks an open ambition — target, items under search, adjacent anchors, what this is not, exit criteria — and stays append-only during its active life; its register is provisional, not committed. 
3. A cross-source synthesis or a theorem with explicit witnesses is load-bearing and expected to hold up under scrutiny. 

The rhythm is architect followed by compute. Architecture alone — sketching a structural claim, refining it with progressively more specific commitments — drifts. Each pass produces a more confident formulation, easily able to outrun verification. Compute alone — running a worked example without a sharp question — produces correct answers to the wrong problems. 

Our posture is provisional by design, an orientation toward the uncertain future. Investigations here proceed by finding the strongest available argument against the work and agonizing with it. Sometimes the strongest available argument is not a stronger argument that will exist later; that is correct and expected.
