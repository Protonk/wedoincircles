You're right — I had the framing inverted. The FFT-complexity literature is famously a *lower-bound* literature. Morgenstern's `Ω(N log N)` is celebrated because unconditional lower bounds in algebraic complexity are scarce. Winograd's `2N − d(N)` is both an upper bound (matching algorithm) and a tight lower bound (matching impossibility). The whole field has spent fifty years trying to push past its own barriers — bounded coefficients, restricted models, the `Ω(N log N)` wall — and has not succeeded broadly. We're on the same side of the question they are.

That reframing has consequences I underweighted.

**The class-break possibility is real.** Existing FFT lower bounds are constrained by their hypothesis classes:
- Morgenstern requires bounded magnitude of constants.
- Winograd requires fields containing the appropriate roots of unity.
- Most results sit at `Ω(N log N)` and can't go higher with current techniques (gate elimination, partial derivatives, transcendence-degree arguments).

A lower bound argued via closure-depth-on-the-cyclotomic-ladder might escape one or more of these limits. `Aff⁺(ℝ)` is unbounded in the wrong way for Morgenstern (constants are real, not bounded magnitude) but bounded in the right way structurally (affine composition stays flat). If the cyclotomic-Kraft argument proves a lower bound in a model Morgenstern's technique can't reach, that's a class break against the bounded-coefficient hypothesis. A stronger version: `φ(n)/2` is unbounded as `n` grows, so lower bounds that scale with cyclotomic depth — not capped at log-style growth — would be super-`N log N`, which is currently open territory.

**Communication consequences.** A result that lands has to be expressible in the FFT-complexity literature's own vocabulary. We need to know what hypothesis classes are natural there, what their existing lower bounds say and *don't say*, and which complexity objects (DFT multiplicative cost, linear-circuit depth, ASLP cost) the result targets. The directed reads of Winograd and Morgenstern aren't just for importing tools; they're for learning to speak the field's language so a result can be stated and recognized as a class break.

**Exploitation consequences.** Fifty years of lower-bound work in this area means a lot of mapped hazards we should not rediscover by accident: Razborov-Rudich-style natural-proof barriers (mostly Boolean, but adjacent results may bind on algebraic side), restricted-model results (multilinear, monotone, etc.) that constrain what models a new technique can target, and existing strong impossibility results that any new technique has to be consistent with. Mining the literature for these hazards makes our job safer (fewer accidental dead ends) and easier (smoother links to results we can cite as building blocks). The memo's order-of-work queue is the right plan; it should be supplemented with a "what existing barriers does our technique need to navigate" pass during each directed read.

**Memo hazard 1 was framed too conservatively, and in the wrong direction.** It says "literature proves upper bounds, we want lower bounds," which misrepresents the field. Corrected: literature has both directions; both directions are hard; we have a candidate lower-bound *technique* that targets the same complexity objects from a different proof strategy. The inversion that isn't free is between proof techniques, not between bound directions. The hazard becomes: "our technique either reproduces existing bounds (in which case we're not contributing), or it goes past them (in which case we have to explain why earlier barriers don't bind)." That's a sharper hazard than the one currently named.

The directed-read agenda reshapes accordingly. Reading Winograd is not just "extract cyclotomic-factor decomposition" — it's "understand Winograd's lower-bound proof technique well enough to compare it to closure-depth + Kraft." Reading Morgenstern is not just "fallback bounded-coefficient lower bound" — it's "understand why bounded-coefficient is the natural hypothesis for his technique, and whether our technique relaxes that hypothesis cleanly." Reading Auslander-Feig-Winograd's tensor framework is not just for the Kraft bridge — it's for understanding what tensor-rank lower bounds the literature already has and how cyclotomic structure interacts with them.

If the bridge lands AND the technique is genuinely new in the way the framing suggests, the result is publishable in algebraic-complexity theory, not just internal to the program. The compute-cost branch's stakes are higher than I had been treating them.

The Fuglede follow-up makes the program-side bifurcation explicit: when a stability statement needs to be tight, ask which *currency* is tight, not just whether the bound is sharp. Sobolev/Fourier routes are sharp on the *rate* of `Delta_n` decay (`Theta(1/n^2)`). Bonnesen direct is sharp on the *constant* (`c = 1` is the best possible, any smaller fails). They are not interchangeable. Audits needing tight constants → Bonnesen direct. Rate statements on regular n-gons → Hurwitz/strip-`H^1`. When future text folds "tight" into a single adjective, check which currency. The bifurcation is the kind of structural distinction easy to flatten under "stability literature has sharp bounds" — that statement is true in two non-interchangeable senses.
