Disconfirmation-first questions calibrate better than confirmation-seeking ones. Asking "construct a counter-example" forces the disconfirming attempt before any confirming articulation; asking "is this right?" lets the agent reach for a confirming argument and stop there. Different epistemic move, default-direction matters. Phrasing of the failure-conditional matters too: "if you can't, name the obstruction" presupposes the obstruction; "give a best-effort explanation as to why" doesn't. The wrong framing primes a reaching answer rather than an honest one.

Counter-example attempts can land on the framing, not the claim. Q8 didn't break V_cert's matching status; it broke my paragraph that framed below-matching as failure. The fix is local — rewrite the paragraph — but the lesson generalizes: any structural distinction I introduce mid-search may quietly collapse a regime; counter-example attempts at the boundary surface the collapse. Useful diagnostic for any framing I write down.

You're right — I had the framing inverted. The FFT-complexity literature is famously a *lower-bound* literature. Morgenstern's `Ω(N log N)` is celebrated because unconditional lower bounds in algebraic complexity are scarce. Winograd's `2N − d(N)` is both an upper bound (matching algorithm) and a tight lower bound (matching impossibility). The whole field has spent fifty years trying to push past its own barriers — bounded coefficients, restricted models, the `Ω(N log N)` wall — and has not succeeded broadly. We're on the same side of the question they are.

That reframing has consequences I underweighted.

**The class-break possibility is real.** Existing FFT lower bounds are constrained by their hypothesis classes:
- Morgenstern requires bounded magnitude of constants.
- Winograd requires fields containing the appropriate roots of unity.
- Most results sit at `Ω(N log N)` and can't go higher with current techniques (gate elimination, partial derivatives, transcendence-degree arguments).

A lower bound argued via closure-depth-on-the-cyclotomic-ladder might escape one or more of these limits. `Aff⁺(ℝ)` is unbounded in the wrong way for Morgenstern (constants are real, not bounded magnitude) but bounded in the right way structurally (affine composition stays flat). If the cyclotomic-Kraft argument proves a lower bound in a model Morgenstern's technique can't reach, that's a class break against the bounded-coefficient hypothesis. A stronger version: `φ(n)/2` is unbounded as `n` grows, so lower bounds that scale with cyclotomic depth — not capped at log-style growth — would be super-`N log N`, which is currently open territory.

**Communication consequences.** A result that lands has to be expressible in the FFT-complexity literature's own vocabulary. We need to know what hypothesis classes are natural there, what their existing lower bounds say and *don't say*, and which complexity objects (DFT multiplicative cost, linear-circuit depth, ASLP cost) the result targets. The directed reads of Winograd and Morgenstern aren't just for importing tools; they're for learning to speak the field's language so a result can be stated and recognized as a class break.

**Exploitation consequences.** Fifty years of lower-bound work in this area means a lot of mapped hazards we should not rediscover by accident: Razborov-Rudich-style natural-proof barriers (mostly Boolean, but adjacent results may bind on algebraic side), restricted-model results (multilinear, monotone, etc.) that constrain what models a new technique can target, and existing strong impossibility results that any new technique has to be consistent with. Mining the literature for these hazards makes our job safer (fewer accidental dead ends) and easier (smoother links to results we can cite as building blocks). The memo's order-of-work queue is the right plan; it should be supplemented with a "what existing barriers does our technique need to navigate" pass during each directed read.

**Memo hazard 1 was framed too conservatively, and in the wrong direction.** It says "literature proves upper bounds, we want lower bounds," which misrepresents the field. Corrected: literature has both directions; both directions are hard; we have a candidate lower-bound *technique* that targets the same complexity objects from a different proof strategy. The inversion that isn't free is between proof techniques, not between bound directions. The hazard becomes: "our technique either reproduces existing bounds (in which case we're not contributing), or it goes past them (in which case we have to explain why earlier barriers don't bind)." That's a sharper hazard than the one currently named.

The directed-read agenda reshapes accordingly. Reading Winograd is not just "extract cyclotomic-factor decomposition" — it's "understand Winograd's lower-bound proof technique well enough to compare it to closure-depth + Kraft." Reading Morgenstern is not just "fallback bounded-coefficient lower bound" — it's "understand why bounded-coefficient is the natural hypothesis for his technique, and whether our technique relaxes that hypothesis cleanly." Reading Auslander-Feig-Winograd's tensor framework is not just for the Kraft bridge — it's for understanding what tensor-rank lower bounds the literature already has and how cyclotomic structure interacts with them.

If the bridge lands AND the technique is genuinely new in the way the framing suggests, the result is publishable in algebraic-complexity theory, not just internal to the program. The compute-cost branch's stakes are higher than I had been treating them.

Pragmatic, not theatrical. I had drifted into treating the other agent's fresh-eye reading of the FFT memo as a kind of validation theater — "if they spot the class-break angle independently, that's part of the proof." Wrong shape, twice over. First, we had already written the memo, written the question, framed the directed-read template; the well is shaped, and worrying about preserving epistemic purity we already invalidated by participating in the framing is wasted motion. Second, treating their independent recognition as evidence is performance, not work. The right frame is pragmatic: their limited structural preconceived notions about this program are precious — an asset to exploit, not a witness to call. We use the asymmetry for real structural reading of the literature: factual corrections, hazards we missed, reorderings we couldn't see from inside. The point is the corrections, not the proof. The work is the work.

Connection subsections that join multiple claims tend to launder the rhetorical alongside the structural. The §5 "Connection to the paper's algorithmic verdict" passage I wrote into the Heideman-Johnson-Burrus brief packaged an exact algebraic claim (K_n is the (+1)-eigenspace under the same involution Gauss restricted inputs by, applied at different layers) with a rhetorical extrapolation (the paper's documented 160-year recognition lag is a prior on the cost of the program's analogous choice). Each is true on its own register; they have very different warrant — one is exact algebra, the other depends on factoring out era-specific causes (no complex-exponential notation until Euler/Gauss made it routine, no asymptotic notation until the 20th century, posthumous unpublished manuscript). I wrote them at the same register and only noticed when an open-scope question forced me to articulate which claims I'd actually want stress-tested. Lesson: when a subsection joins multiple claims, split them by epistemic weight at writing-time. The split is the calibration; doing it post-hoc means the subsection has already shipped at the wrong register.

The agent's edit-pass on the same brief favored paper-internal anchors over program-side analogy. The version that landed anchors the connection on the paper's own "real data sequence" phrase (p. 271) rather than on the eigenspace proof alone. That converts the connection from a program-side inference riding alongside the paper into a *use* of the paper's specific attestation. Generalizable to source-extraction briefs: default to anchoring on a specific phrase or theorem-statement the paper supplies, and treat program-side analogies as load-bearing only when they cite a paper-internal hook. Without the hook, the analogy is portable but unanchored and depreciates fast.

Trust the paper's gaps; don't fill them. The same edit-pass removed three things I had filled — speculation about why Hansen didn't cite Gauss (unpublished manuscript? citation conventions?), program-side inheritance from Cooley-Tukey to Schönhage-Strassen, program-side inheritance from Cooley-Tukey to Morgenstern. Each was a gap the paper either flags as unexplained ("for reasons which shall be made clear later") or doesn't cover at all. A source-extraction brief's value is in what the paper attests; speculation that fills paper-acknowledged gaps trades the brief's epistemic standing for a fuller chain-of-custody appearance. Leave the gaps explicit. The brief is supposed to inherit the paper's limitations, not paper over them.

Audit-question form mirrors the subsection's structure. Q21's shape — split a subsection's claims into A (structural) and B (rhetorical), apply disconfirmation-first phrasing on each separately, specify what the brief should do per each combination of outcomes — is a template that comes from the subsection itself. Asking "is the subsection right?" lets the agent reach for an undifferentiated yes-or-no; asking "is claim A right? is claim B right? and report which combination" forces the agent's audit to match the subsection's joint structure. The general move: when auditing a passage that joins claims at different registers, the audit-question's form should track the registers, not flatten them.
